A heat pump is the system you'll find behind most of the newer homes in Indian Trail — neighborhoods like Shiloh Trace and Chestnut Place went up with them because they handle our mild Piedmont winters efficiently, pulling heat from the outside air instead of burning fuel. When one acts up, though, it doesn't fail like a straight AC or a furnace, and that's exactly where the diagnosis earns its keep.

Before we go further, here's the promise: we don't profit by scaring you. The big-name companies that charge a fee just to show up tend to find a reason you need a whole new unit. We'd rather fix the reversing valve and have you call us again in five years.

The heat-pump calls we see most:

- **Heats but won't cool, or cools but won't heat** — a stuck or failed reversing valve, the component that reverses refrigerant flow between modes.
- **Outdoor unit frozen solid** — a defrost-cycle fault: a bad defrost board, a failed sensor, or a reversing valve leaving the coil iced in cold weather.
- **Runs nonstop and never satisfies** — low refrigerant from a leak, which drops capacity in both modes, or auxiliary heat that isn't engaging on schedule.
- **Strip heat running constantly** — usually a charge problem or a control fault forcing the expensive backup heat to carry the whole load, and you feel it on the power bill.

## Repair It, or Plan a Replacement?

Heat pumps generally give you 12 to 15 years. If yours is in that range and the compressor or the reversing valve has gone — the two priciest parts in the system — we'll set the repair cost beside a replacement cost so you can see the math instead of just hearing an opinion. A newer unit on its first fault almost always gets repaired; a tired one facing a major part is a conversation worth having honestly.
