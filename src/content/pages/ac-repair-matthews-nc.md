It's a July afternoon, the air from the vents has gone lukewarm, and the AC sitting beside your Sardis Forest ranch is the same one that came with the house in the 1980s. In Matthews, that's a common story — and the right first question isn't "how much for a new system," it's "what actually failed, and is it worth fixing." We answer that one before anything else.

You've likely met the alternative: a company that charges a fee just to show up, takes one look at the age of the unit, and quotes a full replacement on the spot. We don't work that way. If a $30 capacitor brings it back to life, that's what you'll hear from us.

The AC calls we run most across Matthews:

- **Blowing warm** — usually a failed run capacitor or a contactor that won't pull in, sometimes a tripped breaker at the condenser.
- **Cooling, but weakly** — low refrigerant from a leak, a condenser coil packed with debris, or a dirty evaporator choking airflow.
- **Iced-over lines** — almost always low airflow or low charge; we find the cause instead of just thawing it.
- **Dead at the disconnect** — a bad control board, a thermostat fault, or a blown low-voltage fuse.

We stock capacitors, contactors, fan motors, and refrigerant on the truck, so the typical repair finishes on the first visit.

## The R-22 Question on Older Matthews Systems

Here's where the age of these neighborhoods matters. A lot of Matthews homes still run R-22 systems, and since that refrigerant was phased out of production, recharging a leaky one has gotten expensive. If your old unit just needs a capacitor, we'll fix it and move on. But if it's lost its charge through a leak, we'll be straight with you: putting costly refrigerant into a 20-year-old system is often throwing good money after bad, and we'll set a replacement number beside the repair so you can choose with the math in front of you.
