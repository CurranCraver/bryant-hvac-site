Heat pumps are everywhere in Matthews, and in the established neighborhoods like Brighton and Habersham, a lot of them have been carrying heating and cooling duty for well over a decade. They're efficient in our mild Piedmont winters, but when one acts up it doesn't fail like a straight AC or a furnace — and on an older unit, the diagnosis decides whether you're looking at a quick fix or a bigger decision.

Here's the promise up front: we don't profit by scaring you. A company that charges a fee just to show up and sees an aging heat pump has every reason to find a reason you need a new one. We'd rather fix the reversing valve and have you call us again in a few years.

The heat-pump calls we see most:

- **Heats but won't cool, or the reverse** — a stuck or failed reversing valve, the part that reverses refrigerant flow between modes.
- **Outdoor unit frozen solid** — a defrost-cycle fault: a bad defrost board, a failed sensor, or a reversing valve leaving the coil iced.
- **Runs nonstop, never satisfies** — low refrigerant from a leak, which drops capacity in both modes, or auxiliary heat that isn't engaging on schedule.
- **Strip heat running constantly** — usually a charge or control fault forcing the expensive backup heat to carry the load, and you feel it on the power bill.

## Repair It, or Plan a Replacement?

Heat pumps generally give you 12 to 15 years. If yours is in that range and the compressor or the reversing valve has failed — the two priciest parts — we'll set the repair cost beside a replacement cost so you can see the math instead of just hearing an opinion. On the older R-22 systems still out there in Matthews, a refrigerant leak tilts that math further toward replacement, and we'll say so plainly.
